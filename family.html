The Lassiter Family Legacy

Visio et Veritas

Marietta, Georgia • 1852 - Present

Introduction: Pioneers of Cobb County

The Lassiter family holds a significant and longstanding position in the historical landscape of Marietta, Georgia, and the broader community of Cobb County. Tracing their roots from the late 19th century into the 20th—and even 21st—centuries, the Lassiters emerged from agricultural backgrounds and gradually became influential through civic engagement, education, and property ownership.

Central to this legacy is Leamond Narvel Lassiter (1879–1966), whose life story embodies the transformation of rural Georgia into a thriving suburban community. The Lassiter family lived through Reconstruction, the Jim Crow era, and major social and economic shifts that shaped modern Marietta.

Genealogical Overview

The Lassiter Lineage: Documented Through History

The Lassiter surname in the United States traces its origins to early English settlers, and by the 19th century, Lassiter families had appeared throughout the American South. In Georgia, records suggest the Lassiters established roots in Cobb County by the late 1800s.

Died: February 17, 1966, Cobb County, Georgia

Federal Census Records: A Window into Family Life

Federal census records from 1900 to 1940 provide invaluable snapshots of the Lassiter family's evolution, documenting their transition from agricultural roots to urban prosperity. These records reveal occupational shifts, household compositions, educational achievements, and the family's adaptation to changing economic conditions.

1900 United States Census

The dawn of the 20th century found the Lassiters in Cobb County. Leamond N. Lassiter appears as a 21-year-old living in his parents' household. William Tom Lassiter, the head, is documented as a farmer—reflecting the agricultural foundation of the family's prosperity.

1910 & 1920 Census Records

By 1910, Leamond had married and established his own household in Marietta. These censuses reveal the family's continued dependence on farming while beginning to diversify into various trades as the county urbanized. The household grew with the addition of children, documenting the expansion of the Lassiter lineage.

1930 & 1940 Census: Urban Transition

These decades, spanning the Great Depression and post-Depression recovery, show significant occupational evolution. The Lassiter family's occupational patterns included teaching, railroad work, and civic affairs. Rising literacy and educational attainment among descendants signified increased community mobility and prosperity.

Federal censuses from 1900–1940 chart an arc of economic resilience, adaptation, and increasing civic engagement for the Lassiter family, showcasing their sustained impact on Marietta's demographic profile.

Historical Context

The Lassiter family's presence in Marietta, Georgia, spans over a century, with documented activities from the late 19th century through the 20th century. Their story is intertwined with the broader historical developments of Cobb County, including post-Civil War reconstruction, the Great Migration, and 20th-century urban development.

Family Members and Relationships

Leamond N. Lassiter (1879-1966)

Born on March 10, 1879, in Georgia, Leamond N. Lassiter was a central figure in the Lassiter family. He married Minnie Lee Brown (1882-1952) in the early 1900s, and they had several children together. The family was deeply rooted in Marietta's African American community, with Leamond working as a laborer and later as a farmer.

Minnie Lee Brown Lassiter (1882-1952)

Born in Georgia, Minnie was the wife of Leamond N. Lassiter. She was a homemaker and played a significant role in maintaining family connections and traditions.

Residential History

1900-1920: Early Residences

In the early 20th century, the Lassiter family lived in various locations in Marietta, including:

  • 1900: Residing in Cobb County with parents William T. and Estelle Lassiter
  • 1910: Living on Washington Avenue in Marietta
  • 1920: Residing on Lemon Street in Marietta

1930-1950: Property Ownership

By the 1930s, the Lassiter family had acquired significant property in Marietta, including land in Land Lot 1042, 7th District, 2nd Section of Cobb County. Property records from 1934-1935 document transactions involving Leamond N. Lassiter and other family members.

Property Records and Land Ownership

The Lassiter family's property holdings were concentrated in Land Lot 1042, 7th District, 2nd Section of Cobb County. Key transactions include:

Census Records Analysis

1900 Census

Leamond N. Lassiter appears as a 21-year-old living with his parents, William T. and Estelle Lassiter, in Cobb County. His occupation is listed as a farm laborer.

1910 Census

Leamond and Minnie Lassiter are listed as living on Washington Avenue in Marietta with their children. Leamond's occupation is listed as a laborer in a brick yard.

1920 Census

The family is recorded as living on Lemon Street in Marietta. Leamond is listed as a laborer in a lumber yard.

City Directory Listings

City directories from the 1920s through the 1940s document the Lassiter family's presence in Marietta, with addresses including:

  • 1927: 207 Lemon Street
  • 1935: 207 Lemon Street (Leamond N. Lassiter, laborer)
  • 1941: 207 Lemon Street (Leamond N. Lassiter, farmer)
  • 1947: 207 Lemon Street (Leamond N. Lassiter, farmer)

Marriage and Family Connections

Marriage records from Cobb County document the union of Leamond N. Lassiter and Minnie Lee Brown, as well as other family connections. The Lassiter family was part of a network of African American families in Marietta, including the Brown, Grogan, and Lemon families.

Community Involvement and Legacy

The Lassiter family was active in the Marietta community, with connections to local churches, schools, and civic organizations. Their long-term residence on Lemon Street (now part of the historic African American Lemon Street District) reflects their deep roots in the community.

Conclusion

The Lassiter family's history in Marietta, Georgia, provides valuable insights into the African American experience in the post-Civil War South. Through property ownership, community involvement, and family continuity, the Lassiters established a lasting legacy in Cobb County. This research serves as a foundation for further study of the family's contributions to Marietta's history and development.

Research Notes and Methodology

This report is based on primary source documents, including census records, city directories, property deeds, and marriage records. The research was conducted through the Cobb County Superior Court Clerk's Office, Georgia Archives, and online genealogical resources. Some records may contain discrepancies in names, dates, and other details, which is common in historical documents.
